Building Safety-Critical Software for Autonomous Vehicles

When your software controls a 40-tonne truck at a busy port terminal, "move fast and break things" isn't an option. Safety-critical development demands a fundamentally different engineering mindset.

ISO 26262 defines Automotive Safety Integrity Levels (ASIL A–D) that dictate the rigour required at every stage of development. For perception systems in autonomous vehicles, we typically target ASIL-B to ASIL-D, requiring formal verification, redundant architectures, and exhaustive testing that covers billions of edge cases.

Safety-critical perception systems must never have a single point of failure. This means dual-redundant sensor configurations, independent processing channels, and watchdog systems that can detect and respond to failures within milliseconds. The architecture must degrade gracefully — losing one sensor should reduce capability, not cause a system halt.

Real-world testing alone cannot achieve the coverage required for ASIL-D. We combine hardware-in-the-loop simulation, synthetic data generation, and shadow-mode deployment where new models run alongside production systems without controlling the vehicle. This approach has allowed us to validate against millions of scenarios before a single line of code reaches production.

Key Takeaways

  • ISO 26262 compliance
  • Redundant architectures
  • Formal verification
  • HIL simulation testing
  • Shadow-mode deployment
  • Graceful degradation
